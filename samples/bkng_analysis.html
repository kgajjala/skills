1. Why Does This Company Exist?

Booking Holdings (formerly The Priceline Group) was built on a foundational insight: travel is the world's largest industry, and the friction between travelers and suppliers (hotels, airlines, rental cars, restaurants) is colossal. The company exists to eliminate that friction by becoming the world's most trusted, comprehensive travel marketplace.

Founded in 1997, Booking Holdings today operates five consumer-facing brands — Booking.com (world's largest accommodation platform, 4.4 million properties, 220+ countries), Priceline (U.S. discount travel), Agoda (Asia-Pacific specialist), KAYAK (metasearch), and OpenTable (restaurant reservations) — plus support brands like Rentalcars.com and Momondo. Together, these brands processed $193B in gross bookings in 2025 from over 1.1 billion room nights.

In Buffett terms: Booking is the toll booth on global travel. It sits between 500 million travelers and 4.4 million properties and collects 15–25% of every transaction. The business doesn't own a single hotel room, plane seat, or restaurant table — it is pure platform, pure intermediation, pure information advantage. Its moat is the two-sided network: travelers go where inventory is largest; suppliers go where traveler demand is greatest. That circular reinforcement, built over 28 years, is extraordinarily hard to dismantle.

The company's evolving strategic vision — the "Connected Trip" — is to move from a hotels-only booking engine to an end-to-end AI-powered travel platform that books flights, trains, rental cars, experiences, and restaurants, and uses AI to anticipate, manage, and personalize the entire trip lifecycle. This is the agentic AI bet: being the entity that executes the trip, not just the entity that books the hotel.

The Pivotal Question for This Investment: Does AI disintermediate Booking (travelers book directly via AI agents, bypassing the OTA and its 15–25% take rate) — or does AI amplify Booking (the complexity of global travel fulfillment, payments, dispute resolution, and supplier relationships makes Booking MORE necessary, not less)? This question is responsible for a ~25% stock drawdown from highs and a multiple at a decade-low. It is the Mauboussin behavioral inefficiency creating the opportunity.

Business Model Transition — Agency to Merchant: Historically, Booking operated as an "agency" — collecting commission from hotels for referring bookings. The shift to "merchant model" means Booking collects the full payment from travelers and remits to hotels. At 68% of gross bookings on the merchant model (2025), this: (1) improves customer loyalty and data ownership; (2) enables "Connected Trip" bundling (pay once, get flights + hotel + car); (3) reduces price transparency that could drive customers to hotel websites; (4) creates more complex accounting (revenue shown gross rather than net) boosting reported revenue. 5. Competitive Moat — Wide But Contested

Morningstar assigns Booking Holdings a "Wide Moat" rating — one of the highest designations in their coverage universe. The evidence supports this, but the AI narrative has created genuine uncertainty about its durability that warrants rigorous analysis.

Moat Components — Genuine and Durable

  • Two-Sided Network Effect (Primary Moat): 4.4 million listed properties joined Booking.com because travelers are there. Travelers go to Booking.com because the inventory is broadest. Each new hotel listing makes the platform more valuable to travelers; each new traveler makes the platform more valuable to hotels. This positive feedback loop — built over 28 years of compounding — is Booking's most durable competitive advantage and cannot be quickly replicated.
  • Global Supplier Relationships (Critical Differentiator): CFO Steenbergen noted at Morgan Stanley TMT that Booking has real-time connectivity to 4.4 million properties and processes payments in 100+ payment forms and 50+ currencies. Building this infrastructure from scratch would take a decade and billions of dollars. This is why Google — despite massive resources — has explicitly stated it will not become a merchant of record or OTA. The fulfillment complexity is a genuine barrier.
  • Genius Loyalty Program (Switching Costs): Level 2+ Genius members representing 50%+ of room nights create economic lock-in — these travelers receive discounts not available elsewhere, and losing that status creates real switching costs. As the merchant model deepens, the loyalty data Booking accumulates (travel patterns, preferences, price sensitivity) creates a personalization advantage that new entrants cannot match.
  • Brand Trust in Crisis: When a traveler's hotel burns down at 2am or a flight is canceled during a geopolitical crisis, they don't want to talk to an AI chatbot — they want to call Booking.com and get a human to fix it. Steenbergen gave the example of the Middle East conflict at Morgan Stanley: Booking helped thousands of travelers cancel, rebook, and get refunds. This trust function is irreplaceable and highly valuable, especially at the high-end travel segment.
  • Scale Advantages in Marketing: Booking spends $6–7B annually on performance marketing — giving it buying power on Google, Meta, and other channels that smaller competitors cannot match. The merchant model is actively reducing this dependency by driving direct/app bookings.

Where the Moat Faces Real Pressure

  • Google (Frenemy Risk): Google Travel, Google Hotel Ads, and Google Flights continue to evolve. Booking disclosed that Google has stated publicly it will not become a merchant of record — but Google's integration of AI search (AI Overviews) into travel queries reduces click-through to OTA sites. This is a slow, grinding headwind to marketing efficiency, not an existential threat.
  • Horizontal AI Agents (The Existential Question): Bernstein's March 2026 note flagged that AI platforms delivering "narrow sets of highly relevant results" via natural language could disrupt the "long list of sponsored links" model. If travelers increasingly use GPT/Gemini to plan AND book trips, and those AI models route to hotels directly (bypassing Booking's commission), the take rate is threatened. The CFO rebutted this: LLM traffic to Booking is "very small and not growing" and most users still book through trusted platforms after using AI for research.
  • Hotel Brand Direct Booking: Marriott, Hilton, IHG, and other major brands have invested heavily in loyalty programs that offer price parity or advantages vs. OTA rates. Under the EU's Digital Markets Act (DMA), Booking can no longer enforce rate parity (blocking hotels from offering cheaper rates on their own sites). This could erode Booking's price advantage in Europe over time.
  • Regulatory Risk (Spain Fine + DMA): A proposed €530M fine from Spanish regulators and the DMA "gatekeeper" designation create ongoing legal and compliance costs. Regulatory scrutiny of Booking's market practices is escalating across multiple geographies.
The "AND" Thesis for Booking — Why AI is More Friend Than Enemy: Booking CFO Steenbergen made the key distinction at Morgan Stanley: he asked an LLM to help him cancel and rebook a flight, and it responded that it was a "travel strategy advisor, not the one pushing the button." The fulfillment gap between AI planning and AI execution is precisely Booking's value proposition. Even in the agentic future, an AI agent planning your trip still needs: (1) real-time inventory from 4.4M properties; (2) payment processing in 50+ currencies; (3) customer service when something goes wrong; (4) trust and liability in regulatory jurisdictions. That is Booking Holdings. Morgan Stanley analyst Brian Nowak made this explicit in the February 24 upgrade: "agents also need BKNG's leading inventory, giving BKNG leverage."

6. Management & Leadership

Glenn Fogel (CEO & President): Fogel has led Booking Holdings since 2017, succeeding Darren Huston. A 20+ year veteran of Priceline/Booking, he was the architect of Booking.com's global expansion strategy including the acquisition of Kayak, OpenTable, and Agoda. Under his tenure, adjusted EPS has compounded at ~20%+ annually. Fogel is an operator's operator — known for financial discipline, shareholder return focus, and strategic patience. He has consistently avoided the "growth at all costs" trap that destroyed Expedia's margins.

Ewout Steenbergen (CFO): Joined in 2022 from S&P Global. Architect of the Transformation Program ($550M in annualized cost savings achieved by year-end 2025 at the high end of targets), the self-funded $700M reinvestment plan, and the "9/9/15" medium-term financial framework. His Morgan Stanley TMT presentation was exceptionally clear-eyed on the AI risk/opportunity — a mark of financial sophistication. The $8.2B returned to shareholders in 2025 reflects a balance sheet management discipline that is rare in travel.

Management Verdict — Exemplary Capital Allocation: The combination of Fogel (strategic vision, deep product knowledge) and Steenbergen (financial discipline, cost optimization) is one of the strongest leadership pairs in the travel sector. Key indicators: (1) Share count reduced 27% in 4 years; (2) $550M Transformation Program at the high end of targets; (3) $700M self-funded reinvestment (AI, Connected Trip, regional expansion) — funded from operational savings, not from earnings dilution; (4) 9.4% dividend increase to $10.50/share; (5) 25-for-1 stock split signals management confidence at current valuations. No governance concerns — single-class share structure, no founder dual-class voting, clean audit history.

The $700M Reinvestment Plan — Return on Investment Framework

Self-Funded Reinvestment Logic: Management has been explicit about the reinvestment structure. The $550M in Transformation savings allows Booking to fund $700M in 2026 reinvestment (AI, Connected Trip, Asia expansion, OpenTable, advertising, fintech) without EBITDA margin contraction. They project this $700M investment to generate approximately $400M in incremental annual revenue — a 57% year-1 payback, with compounding benefits in subsequent years. This is disciplined capital allocation: they're not spending for growth's sake, they're spending on specific initiatives with defined return expectations.

7. Strategic Growth Initiatives

  • Connected Trip Platform: The core strategy — bundling flights (+37% bookings in 2025), rental cars, experiences, and restaurants into a single itinerary that Booking manages end-to-end. As attach rates increase (travelers booking 2+ services per trip), revenue per room night grows without requiring room night growth. This is the key driver of revenue growing faster than room nights.
  • Agentic AI — Internal Efficiency First: Already delivering $10% cost reduction per booking in 2025 via agentic customer service (lower handling time, lower contact rate, maintained satisfaction scores). Management sees more potential in 2026. This is AI as a margin driver — one of the clearest examples of immediate AI ROI in any large-cap company.
  • Agentic AI — Customer-Facing (the Bigger Bet): "Penny" on Priceline, AI concierge on OpenTable, agentic itinerary tools at Booking.com and Agoda. These tools aim to be the AI-native interface that captures travelers before they go to Google or OpenAI. First-mover advantage in agentic travel tools provides brand positioning and data advantages that compound.
  • Asia Pacific Expansion: Agoda's Asia expansion is the highest-growth geographic opportunity. A 20% surge in cross-border APAC travel (2025) combined with underpenetrated OTA adoption in markets like India, Southeast Asia, and Japan creates a runway that European markets don't offer. APAC-specific investment is a key component of the $700M reinvestment.
  • Alternative Accommodations Scaling: 8.6M listings (+8% YoY), representing 36% of room nights, directly competing with Airbnb. While growth is slower than Airbnb's inventory expansion, Booking's supply base covers a wider range of accommodation types (apartments, villas, hotels) and serves business travelers alongside leisure — a more diversified and stickier supply base.
  • Merchant Model Deepening (Payments): At 68% merchant model penetration, Booking is building a fintech layer. Processing payments directly (100+ forms, 50+ currencies) enables better customer data, loyalty integration, and the "pay once for everything" Connected Trip experience. Long term, this payment infrastructure could itself become a revenue stream.
  • OpenTable Expansion: Restaurant AI concierge, expanded geographic coverage, and integration with the Connected Trip ("book hotel + dinner") creates cross-sell opportunities. The $700M reinvestment includes OpenTable growth, signaling management confidence in the dining reservation platform's strategic role.
  • 25-for-1 Stock Split (April 2, 2026): A $4,000+ share price was an accessibility barrier for retail investors, excluding BKNG from price-weighted indices (Dow Jones, for instance, uses price weighting). Post-split at ~$163/share, the stock is accessible to Robinhood users, 401k participants, and index funds that have share-price minimums. Historically, stock splits in high-quality businesses are associated with 6–12 month positive price performance from increased retail participation.

8. Key Risks

  • Agentic AI Disintermediation (Medium-High Impact, Low-Moderate Probability Near-Term): The existential risk. If AI platforms become transactional merchants of record for travel, Booking's commission model is threatened. The CFO's data (LLM traffic "very small and not growing") and OpenAI's stated retreat from direct checkout suggest this risk is further out than feared. But it's real and must be monitored.
  • Google AI Search (Chronic Headwind): AI Overviews reducing hotel search click-through is a slow, sustained drag on marketing efficiency. Booking is responding by building direct/app habits via Genius, but this is a decades-long arms race. Already EBITDA margins are 3%+ below 2015 levels (per Bernstein) from prior Google adaptations.
  • Room Night Growth Deceleration (Priced In): +5–7% room night guidance for 2026 vs. +17% in 2023. Post-pandemic normalization is complete; travel growth returns to GDP+ levels rather than revenge-travel levels. This was the primary driver of the post-earnings drawdown but is now consensus expectation.
  • Regulatory Risk (Ongoing): Spain's €530M proposed fine, EU Digital Markets Act rate parity prohibitions, GDPR data privacy constraints, and multiple jurisdiction antitrust reviews. Booking spends significant capital on legal defense and regulatory compliance. DMA in particular could structurally reduce pricing power in Europe.
  • U.S. Market Softness: Shorter average stays and slight ADR declines in Q4 2025 in the U.S. market. If U.S. consumer spending deteriorates further (recession risk, tariff-driven inflation), the U.S. segment (~18% of revenue) could be a drag. Less critical than for Expedia but worth monitoring.
  • FX Volatility: ~90% of revenue is non-USD, primarily EUR. The $1.17 EUR/USD assumption in 2026 guidance creates ~2ppt revenue boost. A reversal (USD strengthening) would be a 2026 headwind. Currency is always a wildcard for global businesses of this scale.
  • Macro / Travel Demand Shock: A severe recession, pandemic-level health scare, or major geopolitical conflict disrupting air travel would crater room nights and gross bookings. Booking's 2020 performance (-55% revenue) shows the magnitude of a genuine travel shock, though the recovery (to record highs by 2022–2025) also shows the business's resilience.
  • Marketing Efficiency Erosion: Performance marketing was 27% of revenue in 2024, slightly higher in 2025. If Google increases ad prices (as it has historically), the customer acquisition cost rises and EBITDA margins compress. The merchant/direct model is the strategic hedge, but migration takes time.

Key Management & Analyst Commentary

Glenn Fogel (CEO), Q4 2025 Earnings Call (February 18, 2026):

"2025 was another year of strong execution for us. Despite volatility in the broader global markets, the underlying fundamentals of our business are solid. Travel demand remains resilient, and we are building offerings that enhance our value proposition for both customers and suppliers."

"Traffic coming from LLMs to Booking is currently very small and not growing — stable month to month. Morgan Stanley (Brian Nowak, Analyst Upgrade, February 24, 2026):

"We think BKNG and the OTAs are set to be just as important in the agentic world as they have been the past two decades. Agents also need BKNG's leading inventory, giving BKNG leverage. BKNG is well-positioned to own the customer, capture robust traveler data and use those to drive high margin direct business."

Bernstein (Analyst Note, March 2026 — Bear View):

The rise of generative AI threatens to compress margins and erode the "supply moat" — AI platforms deliver narrow, highly relevant results via natural language, which is a more disruptive model than Google's 2010 entry into travel metasearch. OTAs could face meaningful disruption to their take rates and marketing efficiency.
How to Weigh Morgan Stanley vs. Bernstein: Both are credible. The key distinction is timeline. Bernstein's disintermediation concern is a multi-year structural threat; Morgan Stanley's counter is near-term empirical evidence (LLM traffic "not growing"). The near-term investment case (~1–3 years) sits firmly with Morgan Stanley — the current AI risk is behavioral fear, not actual revenue impairment. The long-term case (5–10 years) requires monitoring whether AI agent platforms build the fulfillment complexity to bypass OTAs. Position sizing and exit criteria should reflect this distinction.

12. Mauboussin BAIT Framework — Mispricing Assessment

B

🧠 Behavioral Inefficiency — VERY STRONG SIGNAL

The AI disruption narrative triggered an 8.5% single-day selloff on Q4 2025 earnings — on a quarter that beat consensus on every metric. The stock then recovered 9% after Morgan Stanley's upgrade, demonstrating classic sentiment overreaction. BKNG's multiple is at a 10-year trough despite business performance at or near all-time highs. This is textbook Behavioral inefficiency: investors extrapolating a hypothetical future disruption into the present valuation, creating a dislocation between fundamental value and market price. The April 2 stock split announcement is itself a behavioral catalyst — management is explicitly trying to broaden the shareholder base to counteract sentiment-driven institutional selling.

A

📊 Analytical Inefficiency — STRONG SIGNAL

Most consensus models apply historical P/E or EV/EBITDA multiples without adequately crediting: (1) the 9% annual share count reduction that mechanically drives 15% EPS growth from just 9% revenue growth; (2) the AI cost savings ($10% cost/booking decline in 2025) compounding through the EBITDA line; (3) the Connected Trip attach rate driving revenue per room night above room night growth; (4) the structural demand shift toward experiential travel as a secular tailwind. A simple FCF discount model at current FCF ($9.1B, growing 12–15%) yields intrinsic values of $130–200/share post-split even at conservative terminal multiples — implying current price is at the low end of reasonable value.

I

📡 Informational Asymmetry — MODERATE SIGNAL

The CFO's Morgan Stanley TMT statement that LLM traffic to Booking is "very small and not growing" is the most important piece of information in the AI debate — and it's from primary management commentary, not media paraphrasing. The nuance of the "AND" vs "OR" AI thesis (fulfillment complexity requires OTAs even in an agentic world) requires close reading of earnings transcripts and conference presentations that many investors skip. Similarly, the Transformation Program's $550M in savings enabling self-funded $700M reinvestment is a capital allocation structure that is underappreciated — this company is growing faster while spending more, without diluting shareholders.

T

⚙️ Technical Factor — STRONG SIGNAL

Three converging technical catalysts: (1) Stock split April 2 expands retail accessibility and potential Dow Jones inclusion; (2) $8.2B in 2025 buybacks at $3,800–$5,800 range signals management's valuation confidence — they're buying most aggressively at current levels; (3) Morgan Stanley upgrade created momentum that is likely to attract other upgrades from equal-weight analysts (Bernstein is the outlier at Hold/$4,698). The recent 52-week low at $3,871 provided a technical floor near the bear-case intrinsic value ($3,200–$3,500 range), suggesting limited downside from current levels even in a negative scenario.

The Behavioral fear (AI disruption) is clearly identifiable and empirically unsupported by current data.
